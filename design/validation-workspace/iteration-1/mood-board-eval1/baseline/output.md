# Mood Board: Café Vermut
## Creative Direction Document — Iteration 1 Baseline

**Prepared by:** Creative Director / Brand Strategist
**Date:** 2026-05-13
**Client:** Café Vermut, Barcelona
**Status:** For client presentation

---

## 1. BRIEF SYNTHESIS

### Brand Positioning
Café Vermut is a Mediterranean artisanal coffee bar rooted in Barcelona's Gràcia neighbourhood — a district that has always known how to hold tradition and the contemporary in the same hand. The name borrows from the city's most iconic ritual: the Sunday vermut, a slow, social hour that belongs to nobody and everybody. That cultural gesture — unhurried, local, sophisticated without pretension — is the soul of this brand.

### Core Tension to Resolve
The brand must feel **crafted without being rustic**, **modern without being cold**, and **local without being parochial**. The risk on the left is artisanal kitsch (excessive kraft paper, chalkboards, mason jars). The risk on the right is cold specialty-coffee minimalism that alienates the neighbourhood regular. The white space between those two failures is where Café Vermut lives.

### Target Audience Profile
Locals aged 28–45, Gràcia and Eixample. They read *Apartamento* magazine. They know the difference between a washed and a natural process coffee. They have opinions about bread. They do not own a Starbucks loyalty card and never will. They want the barista to know their order by the third visit. They value traceability but would never use that word out loud.

### Reference Brands Decoded

| Admire | Why it matters |
|--------|---------------|
| **Nomad Coffee** (Barcelona) | Proof that local-first and world-class coexist. Clean white packaging with functional labelling (Basora's license-plate system). Sober, confident, zero decoration for its own sake. The brand trusts the coffee to speak. |
| **Bonanza Coffee** (Berlin) | Best Independent Café Europe 2025 runner-up. Transparent, light, open, ingenious, minimalistic — their brief for their own redesign. Shows that warmth doesn't require visual noise. |
| **Satan's Coffee Corner** (Barcelona) | The anti-brand as brand. Radical personality, no concessions, genuine community. Proves that a strong point of view beats demographic targeting. The satanic name is a red herring — the real lesson is conviction. |

| Reject | Why |
|--------|-----|
| **Starbucks** | Corporate warmth: engineered, scaleable, legible from a motorway. The opposite of singular. |
| **Any chain** | Visual language of efficiency over ritual. The brand exists to extract money from time, not to give time back. |

## 2. CULTURAL & AESTHETIC REFERENCES

### 2.1 Spatial References — Award-Winning Interiors

**Origo Café, Sant Antoni (Barcelona) — Isern Serra Studio, 2024**
The most precise spatial reference available for this project. Isern Serra used manually applied ecoclay walls, terracotta-resin floors, and stainless steel work surfaces to create what Designboom called "an honest and simple space that seeks serenity through natural materials and handmade elements." The elongated layout is resolved through a long architectural bread-display bar. Key lesson: a single material decision, applied with total commitment, becomes the identity.
- Source: [Designboom, November 2024](https://www.designboom.com/architecture/isern-serra-terracotta-origo-cafe-interiors-barcelona-spain-11-22-2024/)

**Miners Café, Barcelona — Isern Serra Studio, 2023**
Earlier Isern Serra project demonstrating the same warm-minimalist language applied to a pure coffee context. Interior design and brand visual language in alignment.
- Source: [Designboom, July 2023](https://www.designboom.com/architecture/miners-cafe-barcelona-isern-serra-coffee-shop-07-04-2023/)

**JAÇ Hifi Café, Avinguda Diagonal (Barcelona) — Isern Serra Studio, 2025**
Shows evolution toward sensory layering: music, space, coffee as a unified ritual. Relevant for the experiential positioning of Café Vermut.
- Source: [Designboom, September 2025](https://www.designboom.com/architecture/japanese-listening-bars-isern-serra-jac-hifi-cafe-barcelona-catalonia-spain-09-13-2025/)

### 2.2 Branding References — Award-Winning Identities

**Nomad Coffee — Basora Studio (Barcelona), 2016–2023**
Basora (Laus Awards recipient, judge at Motion Awards 2025) spent seven years building Nomad's identity around a deceptively simple premise: the coffee label as functional document, not decoration. The "license plate" system encodes origin, variety, and process data in a clean grid. The bags stay white. The typography carries all personality. This is the local benchmark for what discipline looks like.
- Source: [Basora Portfolio](https://basora.info/works/)

**Yettu — Tekwani Design Co. — SCA Coffee Design Awards 2025, Branding Winner**
Won Best Branding at the SCA Coffee Design Awards 2025 (World Coffee Expo, Houston). Custom logotype inspired by handmade farming tools and petroglyphs. Color system built around Rani pink and earth tones. Patterns derived from traditional Indian art. Key lesson: cultural depth expressed through a tightly controlled visual system is more powerful than purely aesthetic decisions.
- Source: [Qahwa World — SCA Winners 2025](https://qahwaworld.com/coffee-society/winners-announced-for-the-coffee-design-awards-at-the-2025-specialty-coffee-expo/)

**Ember Goods — SCA Coffee Design Awards 2025, Packaging Winner**
Family-owned roastery, Olympia. Award-winning packaging cited for evoking "gathering around fire with friends." Emotionally resonant without being sentimental. Demonstrates that warmth is a design problem, not just a marketing intention.
- Source: [SCA Coffee Design Awards](https://sca.coffee/coffeedesignawards)

**Noi Café Branding — "Branding That Feels Like a Warm Hug" (Abduzeedo featured)**
Widely cited 2024 editorial reference for Mediterranean-adjacent café identity. Uses clean layouts, uncluttered typography, hand-drawn elements calibrated to prevent coldness without descending into whimsy.
- Source: [Abduzeedo](https://abduzeedo.com/noi-cafe-branding-feels-warm-hug)

**Wim Café — Hanna Karraby & James Paris, 2023**
Featured on It's Nice That for its typographic innovation. Described as "a refreshing typographic take on café branding." Relevant as a counterexample: shows how type-led identity can carry a full brand voice without illustration.
- Source: [It's Nice That, April 2023](https://www.itsnicethat.com/articles/hanna-karraby-james-paris-wim-project-graphic-design-food-drink-260423)

### 2.3 Cultural Context — The Vermut Ritual

Barcelona's vermouth culture underwent a major revival from the 2010s onward. "L'hora del vermut" is a Sunday institution with deep roots — post-church, pre-lunch, unapologetically slow. Young Barcelonans rediscovered it precisely because it resists digitisation: you cannot vermouth alone, in a hurry, staring at a screen. The name "Café Vermut" consciously inhabits this cultural space and promises the same quality of time, delivered through coffee.

The visual language must echo this: Sunday morning light on old tile. A glass on a marble table. The newspaper that does not need to be checked immediately.
- Source: [Barcelona Metropolitan — Vermouth's New Golden Age](https://www.barcelona-metropolitan.com/eating-and-drinking/vermouths-new-golden-age/)

### 2.4 Typography Direction (2025–2026 Context)

The current macro-trend is a **Serif Renaissance**: high-contrast serifs returning to branding after a decade of stripped-back sans-serif identities. Creative Bloq's 2026 trend report frames it as "breaking rules and bringing joy" — type with character, not just function. For artisanal food and beverage brands specifically, signage-driven lettering with bold strokes, soft terminals, and brush-influenced textures is the leading direction. The counter-trend worth watching: hand-cut editorial serifs that look made by a person, not generated by a machine.
- Source: [Creative Bloq 2026 Typography Trends](https://www.creativebloq.com/design/fonts-typography/breaking-rules-and-bringing-joy-top-typography-trends-for-2026)
- Source: [Fontfabric Top 10 Typography Trends 2025](https://www.fontfabric.com/blog/blog/top-typography-trends-2025/)

## 3. THREE VISUAL DIRECTIONS

---

### DIRECTION A — "Llum de Tardor"
*Autumn Light / The Sunday Morning School*

**Concept**
This direction translates the vermut ritual directly into visual language. It is the warmest of the three — but disciplined warmth. Think: late October morning light coming through the window of a bar that has not changed since 1971, but the coffee costs eight euros because it comes from a 200-tree farm in Ethiopia and is worth every cent. The brand does not explain itself. It simply exists, confident.

**Color Palette**
- Primary: Terracotta / `#C2714F` — the colour of Catalan roof tiles, ecoclay walls, the inside of a café cup
- Secondary: Warm Cream / `#F2EBD9` — not white, never white; the colour of old paper, unbleached linen
- Accent: Deep Ochre / `#B8860B` — saffron-adjacent, Mediterranean sun at 4pm
- Neutral: Warm Charcoal / `#2C2620` — almost black, the colour of good espresso in the cup
- Touch: Dusty Sage / `#9CAB8C` — olive grove, used sparingly as a breath of air

**Typography**
- Display: A high-contrast transitional serif — reference: **Canela** (Commercial Type) or **Freight Display** — optical size large, used for headlines and the wordmark. The thick-thin contrast echoes the rim of a ceramic cup.
- Body / Functional: A humanist sans-serif — reference: **Söhne** (Klim Type Foundry) or **Neue Haas Grotesk** at regular weight. Clean, legible, warm without sentimentality.
- Accent / Handwritten: A single, restrained script used only for the logo's secondary lockup — reference: **Cormorant** italic for maximum contrast with the serif display. Not a handwriting font. A calligraphic cut.

**Visual Language**
- Photography: Available light only. Grain. Subject: hands, surfaces, steam, the space between objects. No barista smiling at camera.
- Illustration: None in core identity. Optional: a single-line contour illustration of the Gràcia neighbourhood skyline for secondary materials.
- Pattern / Texture: Ecoclay wall texture used as background in digital contexts. Halftone grain overlay on dark-ground applications.
- Iconography: Minimal. A ceramic cup silhouette, a tile fragment — used at icon scale only.

**Tone of Voice**
Declarative, unhurried. Short sentences. No exclamation marks. "El café de l'hora del vermut." "Fet aquí. Pensat aquí." (Made here. Thought here.)

**Spatial Translation**
Direct reference: Isern Serra's Origo Café. Ecoclay walls. Terracotta floor. Long display bar. Stainless steel as counterpoint to the warmth — precision and care side by side, visually. Natural materials, zero decoration that isn't also functional.

**Stationery / Touchpoint Preview**
- Coffee bag: White kraft with a single terracotta ink stamp containing origin data in Nomad-inspired functional grid. No photography on the bag.
- Cup sleeve: Blank terracotta paper with the wordmark debossed.
- Menu: A4 uncoated stock, set entirely in type. No food photography.
- Social media: Square format, 3:2 photography, consistent warm-toned preset. Typography overlaid at large display size. No Canva templates.

**Who this is for in the client presentation**
The client who says "I want it to feel like it's always been here." The client who would approve an all-type menu card without flinching.

### DIRECTION B — "Editorial Mediterrani"
*Mediterranean Editorial / The Intellectual's Local*

**Concept**
This direction takes its cues from the design language of contemporary independent publishing — think *Apartamento*, *Stack Magazines*, early *Monocle* before it became a lifestyle brand. It is cooler than Direction A but not cold; rigorous but not austere. The vermut ritual here is intellectual: you go to Café Vermut with a book or a friend who has strong opinions. The coffee is a conversation starter. The space is a backdrop for thinking.

**Color Palette**
- Primary: Off-White / `#F5F0E8` — the colour of a page in a 1970s design book
- Secondary: Ink Black / `#1A1917` — full-body, no pure black (#000000 is for screens)
- Accent 1: Warm Sand / `#D4C4A0` — parchment, ancient stone
- Accent 2: Faded Sienna / `#A0522D` — reduced, almost a stain rather than a colour
- Touch: Pale Verdigris / `#8BA89A` — copper oxidised on a harbour gate

**Typography**
- Display: A large-aperture, editorial serif — reference: **Tiempos Headline** (Klim Type Foundry) or **Eskapade** (Underware). Wide tracking in headlines, used at structural scale. This is the spine of the identity.
- Body: **Untitled Sans** (Klim) or **ABC Favorit** — the workhorse; understated, precise.
- Numeral system: Oldstyle figures throughout. The dates on origin certificates, lot numbers — treated as design elements, not administrative data.

**Visual Language**
- Photography: High contrast, black and white or duotone in Sienna. Subject: process, origin, the hands of the farmers. Coffee as archaeology, not lifestyle.
- Illustration: Technical botanical illustration, precision-drawn in single-weight line. Coffee plant anatomy. Mediterranean herb taxonomy used in flavour notes. Reference: scientific plate illustration, Linnaean tradition.
- Layout: Asymmetric grids. Text bleeds to the edge. Generous negative space used with intention.
- Pattern: A custom repeating mark derived from a cross-section of a coffee cherry — used at tile scale on packaging and environmental graphics.

**Tone of Voice**
Precise, curious, slightly formal. Long-form when it has room to breathe; telegraphic when it doesn't. "Lot #47 — Yirgacheffe, 2025 harvest. Natural process. Notes: bergamot, black fig, dried rose." Flavour notes as prose, not bullet points.

**Spatial Translation**
Reference: Bonanza Coffee Berlin — transparent, light, open, ingenious. A space that feels designed by an architect who drinks coffee seriously. White or light-plaster walls. Exposed concrete used selectively. A large format botanical print as the single decorative gesture. The coffee menu is a printed A3 sheet, reset monthly.

**Who this is for in the client presentation**
The client who says "I want people to think we're serious about what we do." The client who cares about the typography on the menu as much as the font on the logo.

### DIRECTION C — "Carrer i Claustre"
*Street and Cloister / The Living Neighbourhood Brand*

**Concept**
This is the most Barcelona-specific of the three directions. It draws on the visual culture of the city itself: the signage of Eixample pharmacy windows, the tile patterns of Gràcia's street lamps, the announcements painted on shutters in the Raval. It is the most decorative direction — but the decoration is never arbitrary; it always references a real material or cultural source. The brand feels like it grew out of the city, not out of a design brief.

This direction directly answers Satan's Coffee Corner's lesson: a strong point of view beats demographic targeting. Where Satan's uses anti-iconography, Café Vermut uses Barcelona's vernacular visual heritage as its own radical act — asserting that hyperlocal specificity is more powerful than aspirational generic minimalism.

**Color Palette**
- Primary: Gràcia Blue / `#3A5F6F` — the blue of old pharmacy tile, Catalan modernisme ironwork
- Secondary: Limestone White / `#EDE9E0` — the colour of Eixample facades in the afternoon
- Accent 1: Russet Red / `#8B2E1F` — the red of a painted café shutter, faded but present
- Accent 2: Saffron Gold / `#E8A020` — lamp light, ceramic glaze
- Neutral: Iron Grey / `#4A4A45` — old ironwork, shade

**Typography**
- Display: A condensed, slightly vernacular serif — reference: **Placard** (Monotype) or **Reforma** (PampaType). Feels like it was drawn for a sign, not a screen. High on the wall, telling you what this place is.
- Body: **Aktiv Grotesk** or **Acumin** — a neutral foil that lets the display type lead.
- Special use: An all-caps setting for functional information — opening hours, origin data — using an expanded grotesque with tight letter-spacing. Reference: Barcelona Metro signage system.

**Visual Language**
- Photography: Street-level. Tiles in detail. The bar top. The window from outside. Social-documentary aesthetic — as though someone who lives in the neighbourhood also happens to be a photographer.
- Illustration: Custom tile patterns derived from traditional Catalan hydraulic floor tiles (rajoles hidràuliques). Each coffee origin gets its own tile motif, used across packaging and interior.
- Pattern: The tile system is the pattern. It scales from a single coaster to an entire interior floor.
- Colour application: Multi-colour, but never more than three at once. The palette is large but disciplined by restraint rules.

**Tone of Voice**
Neighbourly. Direct address. Barcelona Catalan and Castellano alternating naturally — the code-switch is the identity. "Vine de tant en tant. O cada dia, millor." (Come every now and then. Or every day, better.) "Aquí el café és de debò." (Here the coffee is the real thing.)

**Spatial Translation**
Hand-painted signage on the façade. Hydraulic tile floor inside — a custom pattern, designed with a local artisan, unique to this location. Wooden bar top, old brass fittings. A single chalkboard — but used only for daily origin information, not for cutesy slogans. The decorative richness is in the materials; the graphic identity stays restrained within it.

**Who this is for in the client presentation**
The client who says "I want it to be unmistakably from here." The client who sees the Gràcia neighbourhood as an asset, not just an address.

## 5. RECOMMENDATION

**Primary recommendation: Direction A as foundation, C as influence layer.**

Direction A is the safest path to a brand the client will love and the neighbourhood will trust. It has the clearest references, the most executable system, and the deepest alignment with the stated admiration for Nomad and Bonanza. The risk of blandness in A is countered by borrowing C's conviction about hyperlocal specificity — specifically the Gràcia vernacular tile motif, applied only to secondary touchpoints (floor, coaster, coffee bag label background).

**Secondary recommendation: Direction B for long-term brand evolution.**

If the client's ambition extends to national or international specialty coffee retail — own-brand bags on sale at premium food retailers — Direction B is the positioning that opens that door. It is the identity of a brand that takes itself seriously enough to be taken seriously by others outside Barcelona.

**Avoid:** Building Direction C without full commitment. The tile system, hand-painted signage, and code-switching copy only work as a complete conviction — done halfway, they read as theme-park Barcelona.

## 6. IMMEDIATE NEXT STEPS

1. **Client presentation:** Present all three directions as visual boards with annotated references. Ask client to respond emotionally first (which one feels right, which one feels wrong) before strategic discussion.
2. **Competitor audit:** Full visual audit of all specialty coffee in Gràcia and Sant Antoni (Nomad, Satan's, Parking Coffee, Slow Mov) to confirm differentiation.
3. **Typography test:** Set the café name in the three type systems described. This single exercise typically resolves 60% of direction ambiguity.
4. **Artisan research (for Direction C):** If C advances, identify Barcelona-based hydraulic tile craftsperson (rajoletes artesanals). Lead time on custom tile can be 8–12 weeks.
5. **Photography brief:** Commission a single test shoot in whichever direction advances. Brief: available light, no food styling, no models.
