## PHASE 1 — Research Frame

```
## Research Frame

Client niche:         Food & Drink / Café
Sub-niche:            Artisanal specialty coffee, Mediterranean café culture
Geographic market:    Local / Barcelona — hyper-contextual Gràcia neighborhood
Target audience:      Locals aged 28–45 who value coffee origin, craft, and authenticity
Desired emotional response: Warmth, trust, curiosity about origin, quiet pride
Client tone:          Artisanal, warm, modern — NOT hipster, NOT chain
Brands they admire:   Nomad Coffee (BCN), Bonanza Coffee (Berlin), Satan's Coffee Corner (BCN)
Brands they dislike:  Starbucks and any chain — avoid: corporate cleanliness, mass-market green,
                      generic coffee-icon iconography, seasonal promo noise
```

**Reading the admired brands:**
- Nomad Coffee — minimalist, product-first, Scandinavian influence in BCN; clean e-commerce with single-origin storytelling
- Bonanza Coffee — warm Berlin craft aesthetic; editorial, community-forward, analog feel in a digital medium
- Satan's Coffee Corner — irreverent personality, local cult following, bold character without being loud; pastel + minimal Gothic Quarter sensibility

**What these three share:** restraint, specificity, authenticity over polish, a strong point of view. None of them look like a café website template.

# Visual Direction A — Llum de Gràcia
*("Light of Gràcia" — the quality of morning light in the neighborhood)*

**One-line concept:** "Mediterranean terroir translated into a digital slow morning."

**Personality:** Warm, editorial, unhurried

**Inspired by:** Tastavents Restaurant (Awwwards HM), Artifact Coffee (SiteInspire), Coffee Collective (Nordic-Mediterranean fusion)

---

## Color Approach
- **Overall palette feeling:** Warm morning light — sand, cream, terracotta, deep espresso brown as anchor
- **Dominant color role:** Light airy base (off-white/cream) with warm earthy mid-tones; dark espresso for type and key structural elements
- **Accent use:** A single warm accent (terracotta or amber ochre) used sparingly and architecturally — borders, hover states, single key headlines

## Typography Approach
- **Heading style:** Humanist or transitional serif — something with optical warmth and slight historical resonance (similar to TT Ramillas or Cormorant Garamond); evokes the handwritten notes on a blackboard without being literal
- **Body style:** Humanist sans (like Aktiv Grotesk or Söhne) — warm, legible, not mechanical
- **Weight contrast:** Moderate contrast — headings feel like they were set by hand, body reads with quiet confidence

## Layout Approach
- **Grid feel:** Soft broken grid — mostly structured but with intentional moments where text or image steps out of the column to create organic rhythm
- **Whitespace:** Generous — sections breathe as though air is moving through them; Mediterranean sense of space
- **Section rhythm:** Alternating — story section, then product/menu section, then origin story, then invitation to visit; unhurried pacing

## Imagery Approach
- **Photography tone:** Natural, Mediterranean-specific — warm directional morning light, linen textures, ceramic cups, terracotta surfaces; no professional studio setups
- **Composition:** Mix of close-up textures (coffee grounds, ceramic glaze, wooden surfaces) with environmental medium shots of the space with people in it
- **Treatment:** Natural color grade; slight warmth push in post; no grain overlay, no filters — honest and real

## Motion Approach
- **Animation character:** Subtle and refined — elements appear as though settling rather than entering; slow fade-reveal on scroll
- **Scroll behavior:** Smooth parallax on photography sections only; section-to-section transitions via gentle opacity; no snapping

## What This Direction Communicates
A visitor arrives at this site and feels the temperature drop to pleasant, the pace slow, the morning expand. They are invited into a ritual, not presented with a menu. The brand communicates that it knows what a good morning is, and that it can give you one — in Gràcia, with coffee made by people who care where it came from.

## Best-fit audience
The core local regular (28–40) who already has a café they love and needs to understand what makes Café Vermut worth switching to, or adding. This direction speaks to established taste, not discovery curiosity.

## Risk / Trade-off
The warmth and restraint read as premium and thoughtful, but the site may feel too calm — without a bold typographic moment or strong visual hook, the first scroll could fail to arrest attention in a competitive social media referral context.

# Visual Direction B — Raval Fosc
*("Dark Gràcia" — the evening identity; the Vermut hour as the hero moment)*

**One-line concept:** "The bar stays open in your mind — dark, alive, specific."

**Personality:** Assured, atmospheric, culturally rooted

**Inspired by:** Assembly Specialty Coffee (Awwwards HM 2025), Amrit Palace (Awwwards SOTD 2025), Soyuz Coffee Roasting (Awwwards SOTD + Red Dot)

---

## Color Approach
- **Overall palette feeling:** Deep near-black base with a single warm amber or red-clay accent; cream type for readability; moments of warmth surfacing from darkness
- **Dominant color role:** Dark background (not pure black — a warm very-dark brown-gray like #1C1812) as the dominant base; brand accent as the single light source
- **Accent use:** One strong accent — amber (#C4813A), ochre gold, or deep terracotta — used architecturally; this color should appear in fewer than 20% of the visual space but carry 80% of the brand personality

## Typography Approach
- **Heading style:** Bold editorial display serif or high-contrast didone — the kind of type that looks good gold-on-dark; confident, not decorative
- **Body style:** Clean geometric sans in a light weight — crisp against the dark base; maximum legibility
- **Weight contrast:** High — the contrast between the heavy display and the delicate body creates visual drama and suggests a brand with strong opinions expressed with calm precision

## Layout Approach
- **Grid feel:** Strict grid with full-bleed photography sections as structural interruptions — the grid is the discipline, the photography is the breath
- **Whitespace:** Theatrical emptiness — sections of pure dark with a single line of type; the space itself carries meaning
- **Section rhythm:** Irregular and editorial — long atmospheric section, short copy section, then product card cluster; mirrors the rhythm of a good conversation

## Imagery Approach
- **Photography tone:** Moodily lit — warm point sources of light on coffee/vermut equipment; shadow is narrative, not failure; the "golden hour in a cave" quality of good bar photography
- **Composition:** Mix of tight product close-ups (coffee crema, vermut glass, copper detail) with wide atmospheric interior shots that feature the bar as protagonist
- **Treatment:** Warm grade; +10 contrast; slight Velvia saturation on warm tones; suppressed cool tones; the effect should feel like candlelight seen through excellent glass

## Motion Approach
- **Animation character:** Cinematic and slow — elements emerge from darkness as though lit from within; scroll-triggered reveals that feel like a dimmer being turned up
- **Scroll behavior:** Masked reveals (text slides from behind an image boundary); slow horizontal parallax on key sections; section transitions via cross-fade

## What This Direction Communicates
The brand says: we are open in the morning for your coffee, and we are the reason you linger past noon. This is a café that exists in both the dawn and the dusk. The digital experience feels like entering a space that has been designed by people who take their work seriously and their pleasure equally so — warm, specific, alive.

## Best-fit audience
The 35–45 cultural-professional local who values aesthetic experience as a form of identity. The person who would notice the typography. The person who brings out-of-town guests here specifically. Also aligned with the "admirer of Satan's Coffee Corner" persona — someone who values irreverence when it is earned.

## Risk / Trade-off
The dark base is a deliberate aesthetic risk — a segment of the core local audience (especially those visiting from referral on a bright phone screen outdoors) may find the design harder to scan quickly. Menu legibility in the dark theme requires extra care.

# Visual Direction C — Orígens
*("Origins" — the provenance story as the entire brand premise)*

**One-line concept:** "Every cup has a passport. We show you where it's been."

**Personality:** Curious, honest, quietly activist

**Inspired by:** Ceremony Coffee Roasters (Awwwards SOTD), Nomad Coffee (BCN reference brand), Red Rooster Coffee (SiteInspire), Coffee Collective (Nordic model)

---

## Color Approach
- **Overall palette feeling:** Clean, documentary — warm white base, natural earth mid-tones (sand, dried grass, clay), ink-dark type; the palette of a well-designed book about food
- **Dominant color role:** Off-white / warm paper tone as the base; one natural mid-tone (olive, sage, or warm tan) as the secondary; near-black as the typographic anchor
- **Accent use:** Monochromatic — the accent is the same family as the mid-tone, just more saturated; no complementary color contrast; cohesion over dynamism

## Typography Approach
- **Heading style:** Large, confident grotesque (geometric or humanist) — the kind that works for editorial magazine covers; Freight Display, Canela, or ABC Diatype; conveys conviction without decoration
- **Body style:** Complementary serif for long-form origin stories and producer descriptions; when the content is narrative, the type should honor it with a reading-friendly serif
- **Weight contrast:** Unified weight within categories — all display at one size and weight, all body at one size; the hierarchy comes from scale and serif/sans contrast, not weight variation

## Layout Approach
- **Grid feel:** Single-column editorial for storytelling sections; tight 3-column for product/origin grids; the sections change register as deliberately as a magazine changing articles
- **Whitespace:** Dense information with structured breathing room — the site should feel like it has a lot to say and is organized enough that you want to hear it
- **Section rhythm:** Consistent — the pattern "story / product / origin detail / next story" repeats with satisfying predictability; this is a site you visit multiple times and it rewards each visit with something new to read

## Imagery Approach
- **Photography tone:** Documentary and honest — not styled, but well-composed; the aesthetic of a good photojournalist who happens to love coffee; could be shot on film or with a digital camera set to film simulation
- **Composition:** Environmental: producer in their landscape, barista at work, beans in context; people are always present — coffee is never orphaned without human hands
- **Treatment:** Slight desaturation except on warm tones (skin, earth, wood stay warm); sky and shadow go slightly cooler; the effect is "real world, remembered fondly"

## Motion Approach
- **Animation character:** Snappy and digital but restrained — interactions feel like well-engineered software, not a cinematic experience; hover states reveal producer information as structured data
- **Scroll behavior:** Simple fade reveals and section entrance animations; no parallax; the content carries the experience, not the motion

## What This Direction Communicates
This is a brand that knows where every coffee comes from and considers that knowledge to be the product. The visitor feels they are being trusted with real information — not marketed at, but invited into genuine knowledge. It communicates that Café Vermut is a place run by people with standards, for people who share them.

## Best-fit audience
The 30–45 coffee-origin-curious local who reads food journalism, follows producers on social, and wants a café that matches their existing values. The person who would buy a ticket to a coffee cupping. The persona most likely to become a weekly regular and bring others.

## Risk / Trade-off
This direction risks feeling more like an e-commerce roastery than a neighborhood café if the "hospitality first, shop second" hierarchy is not carefully maintained in the UX structure. The editorial density can also overwhelm if the writing is not at the level the design promises.

Every coffee has a producer, a location, a story — and the design makes that information beautiful and easy to read. The palette is documentary: warm paper tones, ink-dark type, the color of dried herbs and good soil. There's a lot of information, but it's organized so well you want to read all of it.

**What it communicates to your local audience:**
"These people know their stuff, and they think I do too."

## Our Recommendation

We recommend **Direction A — Llum de Gràcia**.

**Why:**

1. **Brand archetype alignment.** The three brands Café Vermut admires (Nomad, Bonanza, Satan's Coffee Corner) all share a common quality: warmth expressed through restraint. Direction A synthesizes this better than B or C — it is warm without being decorative, modern without being cold.

2. **Differentiation from direct competitors.** The specialty coffee scene in Barcelona (including Nomad, Satan's, and other Gràcia players) tends toward either extreme minimalism (Nomad) or bold dark aesthetics (Satan's). Direction A occupies the space between them — neither hyper-minimal nor atmospheric-dark — which gives Café Vermut a differentiated visual position in a saturated market.

3. **Resonance with the primary audience.** The 28–40 local audience that values coffee origin responds most strongly to honest photography, warm typography, and the feeling that the brand is speaking peer-to-peer. Direction A achieves this through the Artifact Coffee and Tastavents models: real light, real space, generous breathing room, a tone of voice that assumes you already know what good coffee is.

4. **Scalability and longevity.** Warm, light-based editorial design ages better than trend-driven dark aesthetics. Direction B is stronger today; Direction A will still feel considered in five years.
